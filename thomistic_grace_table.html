✦ THOMISTIC TABLE ✦

Nature Elevated by Grace
Gratia Perficit Naturam

Below is a Thomistic, systematic table showing how grace elevates nature across the full structure of St. Thomas' theology.

All citations are from St. Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae (ST), with biblical references and Catechism references where helpful.

I. Metaphysical / Anthropological

Natural Reality Supernatural Elevation (Grace) Citations
Natural human powers (intellect & will) Infused by grace and theological virtues ST I–II, q.110, a.2 Ezek 36:26-27 "I will give you a new heart"
Natural inclination to know truth Faith gives supernatural assent to divine revelation ST II–II, q.1, a.1 Heb 11:1 "Faith is the assurance of things hoped for"
Natural desire for happiness Grace orders desire to supernatural beatitude ST I–II, q.5, a.5 Ps 16:11 "In your presence is fullness of joy"
Natural freedom of will Grace heals and elevates the will ST I–II, q.109, a.2–3 John 8:36 "If the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed"

II. Virtues (Key Thomistic Structure)

Aquinas' crucial point: The infused virtues do what natural virtues can never do—they order us to God as our supernatural end.
Natural Virtues (Acquired by effort) Supernatural Virtues (Infused by God) Citations
Prudence Gifts of counsel & wisdom ST II–II, q.52; q.45 James 1:5 "If any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask God"
Justice Charity perfects justice ST II–II, q.23, a.7 Matt 5:20 "Unless your righteousness exceeds..."
Fortitude Fortitude perfected by gift of fortitude ST II–II, q.123; q.139 Phil 4:13 "I can do all things through Christ"
Temperance Perfected by gift of fear of the Lord ST II–II, q.19 Prov 1:7 "Fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge"
Acquired moral virtues The infused moral virtues (same matter, higher form) ST I–II, q.63, a.3–4 2 Pet 1:3-4 "His divine power has granted us...partakers of the divine nature"
Natural religious sense Theological virtues: Faith, Hope, Charity ST I–II, q.62, a.1–4 1 Cor 13:13 "So faith, hope, and love abide, these three"

III. Human Acts (Actus Humani vs. Actus Humanus in Grace)

Natural Human Act Supernatural Act in Grace Citations
Natural love Supernatural charity (love with God's own love) ST II–II, q.23, a.2 Rom 5:5 "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit"
Natural prayer Infused prayer; contemplation via the Holy Spirit ST II–II, q.83; q.180–182 Rom 8:26 "The Spirit helps us in our weakness...intercedes for us"
Natural repentance Contrition (supernatural sorrow) ST III, q.85, a.2 2 Cor 7:10 "Godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation"
Natural obedience Evangelical obedience in Christ ST II–II, q.104 Phil 2:8 "He became obedient to the point of death"

IV. Ends (Purpose and Destiny)

This is the core of Aquinas' doctrine: nature's capacities are ordered to grace, but grace exceeds nature infinitely.
Natural End Supernatural End Citations
Natural happiness (imperfect) Beatific Vision (perfect happiness) ST I–II, q.3, a.8 1 John 3:2 "We shall see him as he is"
Knowing God through creation Seeing God "as He is" ST I, q.12, a.1 1 Cor 13:12 "Now we see in a mirror dimly, but then face to face"
Moral goodness for flourishing Participation in divine life ST I–II, q.112, a.1 2 Pet 1:4 "Partakers of the divine nature"

V. Social Order

Natural Society Supernatural Society (The Church) Citations
Civil society Mystical Body of Christ ST III, q.8, a.1 1 Cor 12:27 "Now you are the body of Christ"
Natural law-based community Church governed by the New Law of grace ST I–II, q.106, a.1 Jer 31:33 "I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts"
Human friendship Charity creates supernatural friendship with God ST II–II, q.23, a.1 John 15:15 "I have called you friends"

VI. Law (Aquinas' Fourfold Law Structure)

Natural Law Supernatural Evangelical Law (New Law) Citations
Known by reason Given by the Holy Spirit ST I–II, q.106, a.1 John 14:26 "The Holy Spirit...will teach you all things"
Commands minimum virtue Infuses grace to fulfill maximum holiness ST I–II, q.108 Matt 5:48 "Be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect"
Justice Interior transformation ST I–II, q.106, a.2 Ezek 36:26 "A new heart I will give you, and a new spirit I will put within you"
"The New Law is chiefly the grace of the Holy Spirit given to believers." — ST I–II, q.106, a.1

VII. States of Life

Aquinas ranks states of life and clearly teaches: the contemplative life surpasses the active life (ST II–II, q.182).
Natural Goods Supernatural Elevation Citations
Marriage Consecrated virginity/celibacy for the Kingdom ST II–II, q.152, a.3–4 Matt 19:12 "There are eunuchs who have made themselves eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom"
Property/ownership Voluntary poverty (evangelical counsel) ST II–II, q.185, a.6 Matt 19:21 "If you would be perfect, go, sell what you possess"
Natural obedience Religious obedience (higher state) ST II–II, q.186 Heb 13:17 "Obey your leaders and submit to them"
Political leadership Pastoral and spiritual fatherhood ST II–II, q.183–184 1 Pet 5:2-3 "Shepherd the flock of God...being examples to the flock"

VIII. Punishment, Justice, and Mercy

This is where the death penalty discussion fits in Thomistically: by nature, it can be just; by grace, mercy and conversion are a higher good when society can be protected otherwise. Exactly the same logic as: natural marriage is good; evangelical celibacy is higher.
Natural Justice Supernatural Mercy Citations
Proportionate punishment Mercy perfecting justice ST II–II, q.30, a.4 James 2:13 "Mercy triumphs over judgment"
Legitimate capital punishment Higher evangelical preference for conversion and mercy when possible ST II–II, q.64, a.2 + q.106 Ezek 33:11 "I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live"
Retaliation within reason "Overcome evil with good" (supernatural) cf. Romans 12:21 Rom 12:21 "Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good"

✦ Conclusion: The Thomistic Principle ✦

All of Thomism rests on one maxim:

"Grace does not destroy nature, but perfects it."
— ST I, q.1, a.8 ad 2

Everything in this table is an illustration of that single principle.
This table represents the full Thomistic structure of how the Gospel raises us above nature.
